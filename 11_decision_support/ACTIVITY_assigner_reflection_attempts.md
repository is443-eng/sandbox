# Activity Assigner: Reflection Attempts

## Attempt 1
The AI was strong at matching obvious skill alignment, like pairing regulatory and technical work with staff who had relevant backgrounds. It also explained most assignments in a way that made the logic easy to follow. The weak part was rule reliability, because some responses drifted from strict assignment constraints or produced incomplete revisions in the stress test. I would trust it as a draft, but I would manually validate all constraints and review any surprising pairings with a human manager.

## Attempt 2
What worked best was the model's ability to synthesize communication style, domain expertise, and client context into one staffing recommendation. It picked up nuanced factors like stakeholder complexity and pace of work, which is useful for consulting assignments. However, it was less dependable when asked to revise one pairing, and the output formatting became partially incomplete. I would use this output as a starting point only after checking one-to-one client coverage and re-evaluating high-risk assignments.

## Attempt 3
The AI did a good job giving fast, readable staffing options with clear rationale for each staff member. It seemed to weight experience fit and project type heavily, which generally improved assignment quality. Its main limitation was consistency under constraints, especially during follow-up reconsideration where it did not always return a fully updated table. I would keep this workflow for planning meetings, but final staffing decisions should include manual constraint checks and partner review.
